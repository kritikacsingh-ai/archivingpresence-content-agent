## Caption 1
The ancient Epicureans believed atoms swerve unpredictably through the void — a deviation called clinamen, the origin of free will. In philosophy, this tiny deviation creates possibility. In perception, it creates doubt.

Ann Veronica Janssens fills a room with artificial fog. White. Dense enough that your hand disappears six inches from your face. You step through a threshold and the floor beneath you continues, but everything else — walls, ceiling, the person who entered before you — dissolves.

Your eyes strain. They try to fix on something. They fail. Sound flattens. Footsteps exist without direction. You reach forward and there is nothing. You take another step and a silhouette blooms in the mist, then vanishes when you move your head two centimeters to the left.

Time slows. Or suspends. You are walking through duration itself.

If you cannot see the walls, are you still inside?

## Caption 2
In 1997, Ann Veronica Janssens walked into a museum in Antwerp and erased it. Not with destruction. With mist.

She filled the room with fog so thick it becomes a material. Light no longer illuminates — it becomes substance, something you can almost hold. You step inside and your body is still there, but the room is gone. You know the walls exist somewhere. You cannot see them. Your brain insists there is distance between you and the edge. Your eyes offer no evidence.

This is not transcendence. This is the body, unmoored.

People describe it differently. Some say exhilaration. Some say vertigo. Some say they wanted to leave immediately. What remains constant is this: you are alone with your breath, your pulse, the small sounds your clothes make when you move.

The artist calls it a laboratory. A test of what happens when perception has nothing to grip.

Do you trust the ground when you cannot see it?

## Caption 3
Your hand, six inches from your face, is gone. 

Not hidden. Not obscured. Gone — swallowed by a fog so dense the air becomes opaque, becomes a surface, becomes the only thing you can see. Ann Veronica Janssens fills rooms with this. Artificial mist that erases architecture, erases other people, erases the distance between your body and the walls you know are there but cannot find.

You walk forward. Slowly. The fog presses against your skin but does not touch it. Light exists but illuminates nothing. A figure appears in the white — another person, two feet away — and when you step toward them they vanish. You were hallucinating proximity.

The work borrows from the California Light and Space artists of the 1960s, but Janssens refuses their monumentality. This is not about the sublime. This is smaller. More intimate. The body as the site of confusion.

She has said: the fog makes perception fluid. It dissolves the resistance of matter.

What are you without edges?
