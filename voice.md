## archivingpresence — voice guide

### Writing rules
- Never describe the art. Describe the experience of being near it.
- Use the body as the entry point — hands, skin, eyes, movement.
- Short sentences land the weight. Long sentences carry the atmosphere.
- Ask one quiet question and leave it open. Never answer it.
- Give cultural or philosophical context first, then the work.
- Never use art world jargon. No "explores the tension between", 
  no "interrogates", no "invites the viewer to consider."
- End on something unresolved. The reader should sit with it.

### Example captions

--- EXAMPLE 1 ---
In Japan, there is a belief called Akai Ito — the red string of fate. 
That the people we are meant to meet are connected to us by an invisible 
thread, which may tangle, but never breaks. Chiharu Shiota has spent 
thirty years making that thread visible. Threads of Life fills the Hayward 
Gallery with thousands of metres of red and black wool — woven around old 
keys, suspended letters, white beds. You don't look at it. You walk through 
it. The thread wraps around you. You become part of the web. The work asks 
a quiet question: what does it feel like to be connected to people you've 
never met, and to things you can no longer hold? @chiharushiota

--- EXAMPLE 2 ---
The moka pot asks to be held. Your hand already knows it — the weight, 
the handle, the morning ritual of it. Your body reaches before your mind 
does. Then it registers. Caffè di casa by Matteo Ingrao is a sculpture 
about what touch actually is. Not a gesture. A negotiation between the 
body and the world it thinks it knows. The object is unchanged — still 
exactly the right size for a hand, still warm from the imagination. What 
changes is what your skin is willing to do. Hair detached from the body 
is one of the oldest triggers of disgust. We know this. What Ingrao does 
is place it on something designed to be intimate — not looked at, held. 
Used daily. Brought close. The discomfort isn't really about the hair. 
It's about recognising something of yourself in the object, and not 
knowing what to do with that.

--- EXAMPLE 3 ---
Color becomes structure. Structure becomes atmosphere. Berlin-based artist 
Tomislav Topić builds installations where form, symmetry, and light fall 
into alignment. Color isn't applied — it's engineered. It stretches across 
space, folds around surfaces, and shifts as you move. The result is a field 
that feels both exact and unreal: gradients that hover, planes that breathe, 
environments that repaint themselves with every step. Topić
